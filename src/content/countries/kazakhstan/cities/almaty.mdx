## First Impressions

> The moment you step out of the airport and see the Tien Shan wall rising behind the city, you understand — Almaty isn't just a city, it's a basecamp with a skyline.

Almaty hits you in layers. The first thing you notice is the mountains — an unbroken line of snow-capped peaks filling the entire southern horizon, so close they feel like a painted backdrop. The second is the trees. This is one of the greenest cities in Central Asia, a canopy of poplars and elms lining every boulevard, softening the Soviet-era concrete into something almost charming.

Then comes the energy. Almaty was Kazakhstan's capital until 1997, and it still carries itself like the country's main character. The café culture is strong, the restaurant scene is booming, and on weekend evenings, Panfilov Park fills with families, chess players, and teenagers on electric scooters. It's a city that manages to feel both laid-back and ambitious — proudly Central Asian but plugged into global rhythms.

What sealed it for me was the accessibility of nature. I've been to cities that claim proximity to mountains; Almaty actually delivers. A 20-minute bus ride from the center drops you at the trailhead for Shymbulak, a legitimate alpine resort. Big Almaty Lake — a turquoise reservoir at 2,500 meters — is a 40-minute drive. No other city I've visited offers this ratio of urban polish to wilderness access.

<Highlight>
- **Zenkov Cathedral**: A candy-colored wooden cathedral in Panfilov Park — built entirely without nails
- **Green Bazaar**: Central Asia's most photogenic market, overflowing with spices, dried fruits, and horse meat
- **Medeu & Shymbulak**: A high-altitude ice rink and ski resort, 20 minutes from the city center
</Highlight>

<Tip title="Getting Around">
The Almaty Metro is clean, cheap (100 KZT / ~$0.20), and covers the main north-south corridor. For everything else, Yandex Go is your best friend — rides across the city rarely exceed $3. The city is also surprisingly walkable along the east-west avenues, especially between Panfilov Park and the Green Bazaar.
</Tip>

## Mountains & Soviet Relics

Almaty's identity is split between two magnetic poles: the Tien Shan mountains to the south and the Soviet-era city grid to the north. Both are worth exploring deeply.

Start with Panfilov Park, the city's green heart. The Zenkov Cathedral anchors the park — a psychedelically colorful wooden church built in 1907, famously constructed without a single nail. Across the park, the somber WWII memorial and its eternal flame provide a stark counterpoint. From there, walk south along Dostyk Avenue, Almaty's grand boulevard, past the opera house and the brutalist Hotel Kazakhstan (now rebranded but still unmistakably Soviet in silhouette).

The mountain side of town rewards even a half-day commitment. Take the cable car from Medeu — the world's highest outdoor ice-skating rink, sitting at 1,691 meters — up to Shymbulak ski resort at 2,260 meters. In summer, the slopes become hiking trails with panoramic views. In winter, lift tickets are absurdly cheap by European standards (~$15 full day). Either way, you're standing in legitimate alpine terrain within 30 minutes of sipping a flat white downtown.

<Tip title="Big Almaty Lake Day Trip">
Don't miss Big Almaty Lake (Большое Алматинское озеро). Hire a car or join a shared tour (~$15/person). The lake sits at 2,511m with water color that shifts from emerald to turquoise depending on the season. Go early morning for mirror-still reflections. The road is steep and unpaved in sections — a sedan can make it, but slowly.
</Tip>

## The Food Scene

Almaty's food scene is a delicious collision of Kazakh nomadic traditions, Russian comfort food, Uzbek craftsmanship, and Korean-Kazakh (*Koryo-saram*) flavors unique to this part of the world.

The Green Bazaar (Zelyony Bazaar) is ground zero. Wander through pyramids of dried apricots, walls of spices, and stalls selling *kurt* — rock-hard dried yogurt balls that taste like concentrated parmesan. The ready-to-eat section serves fresh *samsa* (lamb pastries from a tandoor), *lagman* (hand-pulled noodle soup), and *plov* (pilaf) ladled from enormous cauldrons.

For sit-down meals, the city punches well above its weight. Korean restaurants serve *kuksi* (cold noodle soup) and *morkovcha* (spicy carrot salad) — dishes born from the Central Asian Korean diaspora that you won't find anywhere else. Upscale Kazakh restaurants reinvent beshbarmak and horse meat dishes with modern plating and excellent local wines from the Turgen valley.

## Day-by-Day

<Itinerary>
- **Day 1: City Core** — Panfilov Park & Zenkov Cathedral → Green Bazaar for lunch → Walk Dostyk Avenue → Central State Museum → Dinner at Zheti Kazyna for beshbarmak.
- **Day 2: Mountains** — Morning bus/taxi to Medeu → Cable car to Shymbulak → Hike the alpine trails → Return for late lunch at Navat → Evening stroll along Arbat (Zhibek Zholy pedestrian street).
- **Day 3: Lake & Culture** — Early morning trip to Big Almaty Lake → Return for lunch at a Korean spot → Kasteyev Art Museum → Kok Tobe hill for sunset views → Farewell dinner in the Samal district.
</Itinerary>

## Logistics

<GettingThere>
Almaty International Airport (ALA) is 15km northeast of the city center. A Yandex Go ride to downtown takes 20-30 minutes and costs around $5-7. There's also Bus 79 to the center (~$0.30). The airport is modern and well-signed in English, Kazakh, and Russian.
</GettingThere>

<Accommodation title="Where I Stayed">
**Samal / Dostyk area** — the best balance of restaurants, parks, and metro access. Mid-range hotels and guesthouses cluster along Dostyk and Abay avenues. For budget travelers, hostels near Panfilov Park offer dorm beds for $8-12/night with great common areas. The area around Arbat (Zhibek Zholy) is also excellent for walkability and nightlife.
</Accommodation>
