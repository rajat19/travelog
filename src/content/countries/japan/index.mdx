## Why Japan?

Japan rewards attention to detail. The beauty is often in the transitions: a quiet shrine approach just off a busy avenue, a train ride that feels perfectly choreographed, or a meal that turns precision into hospitality. 

Traveling through different cities here never feels repetitive. Each place sharpens a different side of the country, from hyper-urban pace to ritual, craft, and stillness. It's a place where even a trip to a convenience store feels like an orchestrated, delightful experience.

## The Rhythm

Japan operates on an unparalleled system of efficiency and politeness. Trains run to the minute, streets are impeccably clean despite a lack of public trash cans, and customer service is an art form.

<Tip title="Mastering the Trains">
Get an ICCard (Suica or Pasmo, physical or on Apple Wallet) as soon as you arrive. They work on all local trains, buses, and even in convenience stores and vending machines across the country. For long distance travel, calculate if the JR Pass is worth it for your itinerary (prices increased recently, so point-to-point tickets might be cheaper).
</Tip>

<Tip title="Luggage Forwarding">
The *Ta-Q-Bin* (Yamato Transport) luggage forwarding service is a game-changer. For around $15-20, you can send your large suitcase from your Tokyo hotel directly to your Kyoto hotel, letting you travel on the Shinkansen with just a daypack.
</Tip>

## What Surprised Me

The quietness of the massive crowds. In Shibuya or Shinjuku, you'll be surrounded by thousands of people, yet the ambient noise level is surprisingly low. Everyone is mindful of their space and impact on others.

## Planning Notes

Japan's distinct seasons dictate the travel experience. Cherry blossom (Sakura) season in late March to mid-April is spectacularly beautiful but intensely crowded and expensive. Autumn foliage (Momiji) around November offers equally stunning warm colors and slightly cooler weather.

<Highlight title="Japan Essentials">
- **Cash is King**: While cards are increasingly accepted, many smaller restaurants, shrines (for amulets and donations), and rural areas strictly require cash.
- **Trash Discipline**: You must carry your trash with you until you find a designated bin (often next to vending machines, or inside convenience stores), and sort it meticulously.
- **Onsen Rules**: If you visit a hot spring, remember to wash thoroughly before entering the water, no bathing suits allowed, and tattoos might require coverage or private baths depending on the facility.
</Highlight>
