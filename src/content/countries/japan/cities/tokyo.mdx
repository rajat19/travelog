## First Impressions

> Tokyo doesn't feel like one city. It feels like dozens of massive, distinct cities stitched together by an impossibly efficient rail network.

Stepping out into Shibuya or Shinjuku for the first time is an exercise in sensory overload. Neon lights stretch dozens of stories into the sky, towering billboards play synchronized ads with booming audio, and tens of thousands of people move with a synchronized, quiet efficiency that boggles the mind.

<Highlight title="Tokyo Must-Do's">
- **Shibuya Scramble**: Join the mass of humanity crossing the world's busiest intersection, ideally at sunset.
- **teamLab Borderless**: Lose yourself in the newly reopened digital art museum in Azabudai Hills.
- **Omoide Yokocho (Memory Lane)**: Grab yakitori and beer in this narrow, smoke-filled post-war alley in Shinjuku.
</Highlight>

## The Old Within the New

Despite its futuristic reputation, Tokyo preserves its history. In Asakusa, the grand Senso-ji temple grounds are filled with the scent of incense and traditional snack vendors. Meanwhile, tucked behind the luxury boutiques of Omotesando, you'll find the serene, forested grounds of Meiji Jingu shrine, so quiet you forget you're in a city of 37 million people.

<Tip title="Transit Etiquette">
On escalators, stand on the left (in Tokyo, unlike Osaka where you stand on the right). On trains, keep your phone on silent and refrain from talking loudly. It creates the peaceful transit environment everyone appreciates.
</Tip>

## Culinary Capital

Tokyo has more Michelin stars than any other city in the world, but the cheap eats are equally impressive. Whether you're sliding a ticket into a ramen vending machine or sitting down for a meticulously crafted omakase sushi experience, the dedication to quality is ubiquitous.

<FoodGuide>
| Dish | Where to Try | Price |
|------|--------------|-------|
| **Tsukemen (Dipping Ramen)** | Rokurinsha (Tokyo Station) | ¥1,000 |
| **Tonkatsu (Fried Pork)** | Tonkatsu Maisen (Aoyama) | ¥3,000 |
| **Conbini Egg Sandwich** | Any 7-Eleven or Lawson | ¥300 |
| **High-end Sushi** | Sushi Dai (Toyosu Market) | ¥5,000 |
</FoodGuide>

<Itinerary>
- **Day 1: West Side Energy** — Shibuya Crossing → Harajuku (Takeshita Street) → Meiji Shrine → Shinjuku for dinner (Omoide Yokocho).
- **Day 2: Tradition & Tech** — Asakusa (Senso-ji Temple) → Akihabara (anime/electronics) → Ueno Park.
- **Day 3: Art & Luxury** — teamLab Borderless (Azabudai Hills) → Ginza shopping → Tsukiji Outer Market for seafood snacks.
- **Day 4: Chill Neighborhoods** — Shimokitazawa (vintage clothes & indie cafes) → Nakameguro (strolling the canal).
- **Day 5: Pop Culture** — Tokyo Skytree → Ikebukuro (Sunshine City) or a day trip to Yokohama.
</Itinerary>

## Logistics

<GettingThere>
Haneda Airport (HND) is much closer to the city center and connects via the monorail in 30 mins. Narita Airport (NRT) takes about an hour via the Narita Express (N'EX) or Skyliner, but often has cheaper flights.
</GettingThere>

<Accommodation title="Where I Stayed">
**Shinjuku**: It serves as an incredible hub. Staying near Shinjuku station meant we could get anywhere in the city directly, and the nightlife options (Golden Gai, Kabukicho) were just a short walk away.
</Accommodation>
