## Why the US?

The United States is too massive to summarize as a single destination. It operates like 50 different countries stitched together by an interstate highway system. 

The draw of the US lies in its scale—you can go from the hyper-dense, towering streets of Manhattan to the otherworldly deserts of the Southwest, to the laid-back surf culture of the California coast, all under one flag.

## The Rhythm

The defining characteristic of American travel is the car. Outside of a handful of northeastern cities (New York, Boston, DC), public transit is lacking. 

American cities are expansive. Renting a car (or relying heavily on Uber/Lyft) is often the only way to genuinely experience places like Los Angeles or Austin. This car-centric culture gave rise to the great American road trip—a ritual of cheap motels, diners, and endless horizons.

<Tip title="Tipping Culture">
Tipping is not optional in the US; it is the primary way service staff make a living. The standard tip in a sit-down restaurant is 18% - 20%. Bartenders expect $1-2 per drink, and tipping bellhops, valets, and drivers is standard.
</Tip>

## What Surprised Me

The sheer size of the portions, the spaces, and the country itself. A "short drive" to an American might be 3 hours. Everything is scaled up, which can be exhausting but also incredibly liberating when you hit the open road.

## Planning Notes

Healthcare is famously expensive. Do not travel to the US without comprehensive travel insurance that covers medical emergencies.

<Highlight title="US Essentials">
- **The Price You See Isn't Final**: Unlike most of the world, tax is NOT included in the listed price. If an item says $10 on the shelf, expect to pay $10.80+ at the register depending on local state taxes.
- **National Parks**: America’s greatest asset is its National Park system. If you plan to visit more than 2-3 parks, buy the "America the Beautiful" annual pass for $80 to save money.
</Highlight>
